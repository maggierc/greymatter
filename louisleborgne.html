Louis Leborgne

Louis Leborgne was hospitalized after he developed a complete inability to speak. Although he could fully understand what people said, he couldn't respond with anything but the word "tan".

Background

Little is known about Louis' background before becoming a patient. We know he was a Frenchman of the mid 1800s.

He lived in Paris as a shoemaker.

when the condition set in.

What Happened

At age 30, Louis began to stutter.

Stuttering quickly turned into broken speech, and eventually Louis couldn't speak at all.

He was unable to work or talk to anybody, and was soon admitted to a hospital. Because of how strange his condition became, he was admitted into the care of one history's most famous neuroscientists: Paul Broca.

What Happened

At age 30, Louis began to stutter.

Stuttering quickly turned into broken speech, and eventually Louis couldn't speak at all.

He was unable to work or talk to anybody, and was soon admitted to a hospital. Because of how strange his condition became, he was admitted into the care of one history's most famous neuroscientists: Paul Broca.

Symptoms

Louis could understand what people said to him, and would try to respond,

but the only word he could ever say was "tan".

Louis was aware of what was happening. He would wave his arms, gesture, jump up and down, but he could never get past "tan".

After 10 years in the hospital, his brain damage began to affect the rest of his body. His right side slowly seized up, and eventually became gangrene.

Symptoms

When Louis was admitted to the hospital, he had a variety of strange symptoms. He could understand what people said to him, and would try to respond,

but the only word he could ever say was "tan".

Louis was aware of what was happening. He would wave his arms, gesture, jump up and down, but he could never get past "tan".

After 10 years in the hospital, his brain damage began to affect the rest of his body. His right side slowly seized up, and eventually became gangrene.

Louis Leborgne's started Paul Broca's famous study that found language to be stored in specific parts of the brain.

Broca's research eventually defined aphasia, a specific kind of brain damage that disrupts language abilities. Aphasia affects around a million in the US, and has vast treatment and research surrounding it now.
